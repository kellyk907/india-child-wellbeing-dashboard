Program evaluation findings

Progress achieved

30-year decline in stunting

Stunting fell from 51.9% (1992) to 35.5% (2021) — a significant decline driven by nutrition programs, sanitation access, and institutional delivery expansion. NFHS-5 shows continued improvement in stunting, underweight, and wasting vs. NFHS-4.

Critical gap

Anemia is rising, not falling

Children under 5 with anemia rose from 58.6% (NFHS-4) to 67.1% (NFHS-5) — the one major indicator moving in the wrong direction. Anemia causes cognitive delays, fatigue, and weakened immunity, undermining gains made in other areas.

Equity concern

National averages hide state-level crises

Stunting ranges from ~20% in Puducherry to 46.5% in Meghalaya and 42.9% in Bihar. National improvement is driven by a few high-performing states masking regression elsewhere — making disaggregated, state-level data essential for program design.

Program lever

Women's empowerment drives outcomes

States with higher rates of women owning mobile phones and accessing bank accounts showed stronger child nutrition improvements — supporting whole-household, family-centered program approaches that invest in mothers' agency alongside child nutrition.

High-priority interventions

Targeted anemia intervention in Bihar, UP, Rajasthan, and Meghalaya — iron supplementation with community health worker delivery
Strengthen frontline worker capacity (ASHAs, AWWs) in high-stunting states through training and supervision support
Rural-focused nutrition programs — rural stunting (37.3%) significantly outpaces urban (30.1%)

Program design principles

Integrate women's economic empowerment into child nutrition program design — these outcomes are directly linked
Use state-level disaggregated data — not national averages — to allocate resources where gaps are widest
Apply intergenerational approach: address maternal nutrition pre- and post-pregnancy alongside child nutrition
